Hedge fund repositioning, disclosed each quarter in 13F filings, carries information about both the direction and the uncertainty of future prices, and the two travel through different channels. Existing holdings-embedding work represents the static identity of a stock from a single cross-section of holdings; we instead embed how a stock's hedge fund ownership changes from quarter to quarter, so the object of study is a repositioning event rather than a classification of what a stock is. Using Partial Least Squares on a 16-dimensional embedding of these holdings changes (2005--2024, 103,663 stock-quarter observations), we recover the directions that best predict post-filing returns and realized volatility. Our organizing question is what survives out of sample. The return channel is strong in sample but earns no reliable out-of-sample alpha, the signature of information that is priced away once positions become public: a clean statement of semi-strong efficiency for the directional content of disclosure. The volatility channel is established through a model-free filing-date event study: stocks whose repositioning is large but ambiguous experience a sharp volume spike on the filing date with no accompanying price move, the volume-without-consensus signature of differential interpretation, followed by elevated realized volatility over the next month. We treat the contrast between in-sample predictability and out-of-sample decay as the central result: it dates how quickly the market incorporates the two kinds of information that hedge fund disclosure reveals.
